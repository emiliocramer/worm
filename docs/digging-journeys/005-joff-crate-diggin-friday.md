# 005 - Joff, Crate Diggin Friday

Source: user-provided article by Joff about Crate Diggin' Friday at Rough Trade
Nottingham and what crate digging means now.

## Digging Journeys

- Pre-internet DJ needs music -> travels to discerning shops, dealers, second
  hand stores, and random shacks -> exchanges knowledge with people who know
  more -> comes back with records other DJs do not have -> DJing and digging are
  the same practice.

- Local shop dig -> spends four hours searching physical crates -> no target,
  no database, no instant download -> distance and patience decide what becomes
  findable.

- Serious DJ wants music outside local reach -> flies to another country ->
  searches second-hand shops and random shacks -> finds lost music because the
  journey goes farther than other people's search.

- DJ does not want to play only latest hits, classics, or one micro-genre ->
  looks across different times and places -> mixes records from outside one
  scene -> DJ set becomes an argument for digging rather than trend-following.

- Early cross-scene DJs and scenes -> David Mancuso loft parties, Northern Soul,
  Daniele Baldelli, Grandmaster Flash, Afrika Bambaataa, Kool Herc -> mix music
  from different origins -> crate digging becomes a way to build a room from
  several histories at once.

- Young DJ in South West England -> hears John Stapleton, Gilles Peterson,
  Mo'Wax, and Ninja Tune -> sees old finds working next to new music -> starts
  mixing old and new behind Drum and Bass nights while genre-specific DJs play
  stricter sets.

- Northern Soul discovery route -> Ian Levine travels to the USA when that is
  rare for working Britain -> finds soul seven-inches in their original context
  -> brings them back to the UK -> a different scene adopts and celebrates them.

- Pre-web rarity route -> someone finds music out of context with very few
  copies around -> scarcity makes the record powerful in a DJ set -> digital
  access later turns the same music into infinite downloads or re-edits -> the
  object stays rare even when the sound does not.

- Modern rarity route -> old rare records become commodities -> digger looks
  instead at unnoticed producers online -> finds a one-off Bandcamp track nobody
  cares about yet -> rarity shifts from scarce object to ignored genius.

- Context-flip route -> a track that was once a mainstream hit enters a new room
  and time -> Kool Herc and block-party DJs use Shirley Ellis' "The Clapping
  Song" as a break -> a 1960s pop song becomes a hip-hop weapon.

- Storytelling route -> DJ searches instead of following trends -> builds a
  pattern, landscape, or collage from records across eras and places -> the room
  hears familiar, obscure, old, new, local, and alien music as one story.

## What Moves The Choice

- Search pressure: needing records other DJs are not playing, wanting to tell a
  different story, refusing latest-hit or micro-genre limits.
- Places: local shops, dealer networks, second-hand stores, random shacks,
  foreign trips, Bandcamp, dance floors.
- People/scenes: knowledgeable dealers, older DJs, Northern Soul, loft parties,
  early hip-hop, South West England crews, Drum and Bass back rooms.
- Cues: rarity, out-of-context music, old records that can work in a new room,
  one-off online tracks, breaks, scene adoption.
- Digital shift: YouTube, Discogs, social media, hard drives, downloads, re-edits
  make music easier to find but change what rarity means.
- Payoff: a record or track becomes newly alive because a DJ moves it into a
  different time, place, room, or story.
