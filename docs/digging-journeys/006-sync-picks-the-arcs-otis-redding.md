# 006 - Sync Picks, The Arcs And Otis Redding

Source: user-provided sync/music-supervision article excerpt about The Arcs'
"Electrophonic Chronic" and Otis Redding's "Live at the Whisky A Go Go".

## Digging Journeys

- Not much of a Black Keys fan -> disappears down a Danger Mouse rabbit hole ->
  discovers Danger Mouse produced The Black Keys' "Attack & Release" -> gives
  Dan Auerbach's catalog a real chance -> a band he had ignored opens into a
  large bank of songs he likes.

- Dan Auerbach door is open -> stumbles across The Arcs' debut "Yours,
  Dreamily" -> sees Auerbach with Leon Michels, Homer Steinweiss, Nick Movshon,
  and Richard Swift -> follows that side-project path -> later lands on
  "Electrophonic Chronic".

- Knows "Electrophonic Chronic" came from sessions revisited after Richard
  Swift's death -> hears warmth, looseness, R&B, blues, grooves, and studio
  experimentation -> the backstory adds grief and time to the listen -> returns
  to the record again and again.

- Works in sync and hears "Sunshine" from "Electrophonic Chronic" -> lyrical
  nods are obviously usable -> instrumental is stripped back, modern, and has a
  hip-hop-like beat -> imagines it working for fashion or film.

- Fan of Otis Redding since playing his songs in a soul band in the early 2000s
  -> remembers trying the "Dock of the Bay" whistle into a microphone and
  learning about feedback -> personal performance history keeps Redding's music
  vivid.

- Played "I'm Depending on You" on saxophone and drums -> remembers bad
  transcriptions, transposed woodwind charts, and endless alto runs -> hears the
  live Whisky A Go Go version -> the recording triggers musician memory instead
  of just artist admiration.

- Listens to "Live at the Whisky A Go Go" -> hears tight live playing without
  click tracks or Ableton-style polish -> each player gets space and nobody
  overplays -> values the record because the performance breathes.

- Matches music to picture for work -> compares live energy with polished studio
  recordings -> hears breathless vocals, ad libs, groove, and vulnerability ->
  concludes that imperfection and live performance can carry more sync emotion
  than polish.

## What Moves The Choice

- Entry points: producer credit, side project, band member network, old
  performance memory, sync use case.
- Cues: Danger Mouse production, Dan Auerbach involvement, Richard Swift
  backstory, live tightness, ad libs, lyrical sync hooks, beat feel.
- Personal filters: previously ignored band, musician experience, working with
  music-to-picture, memories of playing the material.
- Sound filters: warmer and looser than garage rock, relaxed jam feel, R&B,
  blues, groove, stripped-back modern instrumental, live imperfections.
- Payoff: a dismissed artist becomes accessible, a side project becomes a repeat
  listen, a live record proves useful because human performance carries emotion.
