# 007 - Stephen McLeod Blythe, Vinyl Sampling Workflows

Source: Stephen McLeod Blythe, "Crate Digging, Sample Flipping, & Vinyl Sampling
Workflows", 2023-06-30.

## Digging Journeys

- Collects vinyl for over two decades -> only recently understands crate
  digging -> stops seeing dusty albums by forgotten acts as junk -> starts
  hearing them as possible snippets of sonic gold for sampling.

- Has access to YouTube and recorded-music history online -> still spends money
  on unknown physical records -> wants hand-picked source material, tactile
  connection, and the snaps/crackles/pops of the actual journey -> vinyl becomes
  worth the extra effort.

- Virtual digging offers endless resources -> people default to the same online
  pathways -> physical record stores and thrift shops each have different stock
  -> local oddities and a Korea trip surface records he would not find at home or
  online -> serendipity becomes part of the creative process.

- Faces overwhelming bins while hunting for sample material -> filters for
  obscurity, strange artwork, inner-sleeve details, unusual instruments, older
  or poorly recorded albums, instrumental soul/funk/disco/folk/classical, and
  low price -> avoids rock, rap, and Western pop because they are too
  distinctive or dense to lift from.

- Finds possible sample records -> keeps purchases to a few bucks because higher
  prices imply known/desirable records and raise the gamble -> dirt-cheap
  discovery feels more rewarding when it produces an inspiring sound.

- Brings records back to the studio -> adds them to the collection -> avoids
  listening until starting a track -> fresh ears make standout sections easier
  to notice -> samples as he goes so he does not lose the needle position.

- Starts every sample workflow at the turntable -> changes playback speed and
  pitch before recording -> a dull record at 33 1/3 can become usable at another
  speed -> platter pitch becomes part of the sample's character.

- Plays vinyl through a capture device that adds color -> chooses devices like
  Boss SP-202 or Yamaha SU-700 instead of transparent capture -> different bit
  rates and sampler signatures become part of the sound.

- Listens without knowing exactly what he wants -> waits for a chord, vocal
  line, full passage, or melodic moment to jump out -> records it -> shapes it
  with EQ, filters, and FX -> often removes lows or drums to leave room for the
  track he will build.

- Moves the chosen audio into an Akai Force or MPC 2500 -> chops it into
  playable sections -> re-pitches across pads -> uses tap tempo to find a pace
  -> builds melody to a click -> adds manually played drums, sequences, one-shot
  FX, and sometimes synth parts.

- Chooses not to make the whole beat uniformly "lofi" -> pairs gritty melodic
  vinyl samples with cleaner drum sounds -> finishes production and mixing in
  the sampler or multitracks into Logic.

- Builds a setup -> rejects gear mythology and one-right-way thinking -> uses
  whatever turntable, preamp, sampler, recorder, tracker, or grid workflow keeps
  the process inspiring -> vinyl sampling is a route into creativity, not a
  fixed genre.

- Buys vinyl to sample from -> unexpectedly starts listening to and enjoying
  music he would not otherwise hear -> sampling hunt expands his listening taste
  beyond the original production goal.

## What Moves The Choice

- Entry points: sampling need, boredom with digital sameness, tactile process,
  local shop oddities, travel finds.
- Source filters: obscurity, weird art, inner sleeves, unusual instruments,
  older/rougher recording, instrumental passages, cheap LPs.
- Avoided sources: records that are too known, too expensive, too distinctive,
  too dense, or too hard to lift from.
- Freshness tactic: do not listen after buying until making a track; sample on
  first contact so the standout moment is not dulled by repetition.
- Sound-shaping cues: platter speed, pitch, sampler color, bit rate, EQ, filter,
  removing lows/drums, melodic fragments.
- Tools: turntable, phono preamp, Boss SP-202, Yamaha SU-700, Akai Force, MPC
  2500, drum samples, synths, Logic.
- Payoff: source material feels earned, the sample carries physical character,
  and the search opens music the producer would not have found otherwise.
