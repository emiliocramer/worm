# 004 - eMusic And Digital Crate Digging

Source: user-provided article about bargain record hunting and crate-digging for
MP3s through eMusic.

## Digging Journeys

- Longtime bargain-record habit -> checks record stores, highway antique shops,
  second-hand emporiums, old book shops, and Goodwill buildings -> looks through
  crates without a target -> wants a cheap thrill or a way to kill time -> the
  find matters because it was not planned.

- Upstate New York second-hand book shop -> spends $1 on a mint vinyl copy of
  the "Forbidden Planet" soundtrack -> recognizes it as an early electronic
  album made with pre-Moog synthesizers -> cheap physical browsing turns into a
  historically meaningful find.

- West Eighth Street bargain stores -> new jazz overstock is cheap enough at
  three albums for $5 -> judges records by cover and label -> takes chances on
  Yusef Lateef, Cal Tjader, and Pharoah Sanders' "Tauhid" -> low price makes
  risk feel like discovery instead of waste.

- Modern Goodwill browse -> expects there is always something -> mostly finds
  over-donated easy-listening/Christmas records and poorly kept vinyl -> keeps
  browsing anyway -> the hunt survives even when the hit rate is bad.

- Too many streaming choices -> radio or playlist shuffle becomes the easy
  default -> still wants the pleasure of looking for music -> keeps the digging
  behavior but moves it to MP3s.

- eMusic subscription since 2007 -> pays monthly and must use the purchase
  credit before it expires -> has about $14 to spend -> turns the subscription
  into a forced monthly dig for roughly two albums.

- Still thinks albums are the proper unit -> avoids buying only single tracks ->
  searches eMusic for full albums under the monthly credit limit -> uses singles
  only to round out leftover balance.

- Older jazz albums have fewer, longer tracks -> tracks cost around 49 cents ->
  a five- or six-track jazz LP can cost $2.49 or $2.99 -> track pricing makes
  older jazz a higher-yield dig.

- eMusic catalog shrinks after labels disappear -> worthwhile music becomes
  harder to find -> browsing takes more time -> gems still surface -> finds The
  9th Creation's "A Step Ahead" on Past Due -> old soul-funk feels fresh because
  it has been out of rotation.

- Finds Graham Parker and the Rumour concert set -> label has many "Legends
  Live" classic-rock concert discs -> almost no session/date/band-lineup info ->
  decision becomes a gamble based on artist interest and low price.

- Sees HHO label releases across David Crosby and Graham Nash, Captain
  Beefheart, Doc Watson, Duke Pearson, Soft Machine, and Merle Haggard -> label
  provenance is unclear -> uses 30- or 60-second samples before buying -> sample
  preview becomes protection against off-brand or re-recorded material.

- Digs Nagel Heyer jazz catalog -> finds Lou Donaldson's "Night Tide" and an
  Eric Dolphy collection called "Sugar" -> researches track provenance -> learns
  some tracks come from the 1961 "Caribe" session with the Latin Jazz Quintet
  and Eric Dolphy -> buys selected tracks if the whole compilation is not needed.

- Browses Blue Moon Music classic jazz catalog -> sees names like Charles
  Mingus, Wayne Shorter, and Herbie Hancock -> provenance is uncertain -> smart
  move is to web-search sessions before buying.

- Browses Trunk Records -> label specializes in lost film scores, unreleased TV
  music, sexploitation, and kitsch -> pokes around oddities -> finds Miles
  Davis' "Elevator to the Gallows" soundtrack -> weird label lanes can hide
  serious music.

- Checks artist-label catalogs -> finds Rolling Tide carrying Steve Forbert,
  Willie Nile albums, and Righteous Babe still carrying Ani DiFranco -> album
  price is better than buying individual tracks -> catalog availability becomes
  a reason to buy now before it disappears.

- Notices Sonic Youth, Acony Records, Gillian Welch, and Dave Rawlings can vanish
  from eMusic without warning -> monthly browsing becomes partly defensive ->
  if a wanted catalog is there now, waiting may lose it.

## What Moves The Choice

- Price: $1 vinyl, three albums for $5, $6.99 albums, 49-cent tracks, expiring
  monthly credit.
- Format: vinyl, CD-era thinking, MP3 downloads, album-first buying, occasional
  single-track fill-ins.
- Places: record stores, antique shops, second-hand shops, book shops, Goodwill,
  eMusic catalog pages.
- Cues: cover, label, artist name, historical importance, track count, album
  price, sample preview, session provenance.
- Friction: damaged or poorly kept vinyl, too many choices, shrinking catalog,
  missing personnel/session info, possible off-brand or re-recorded material.
- Research loop: preview sample -> web search provenance -> compare track/album
  price -> buy whole album or selected tracks.
- Payoff: cheap thrill, rare soundtrack, jazz overstock discovery, old music made
  fresh, hidden film score, better use of a forced monthly spend.
